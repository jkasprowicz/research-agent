### 2. Missingness/completeness summary

Created:

- `tabela_s1_completude_variaveis_analiticas.csv`

The manuscript now reports final-cohort observation rates for the main clustering variables, showing that:

- hematologic variables are almost fully observed
- creatinine has high direct availability
- PCR and transaminases remain available in most, but not all, encounters

This strengthens transparency around imputation dependence.

### 3. Deeper sex-based phenotype interpretation

The Results and Discussion now give the sex redistribution scientific weight rather than treating it as a side note.

The revised text emphasizes:

- male enrichment in the inflammatory-renal phenotype
- female enrichment in the basal phenotype
- the possibility that the latent structure reflects meaningful biological and clinical heterogeneity rather than only geometric partitioning

### 4. Stronger basal phenotype interpretation

The basal phenotype is now described more positively and specifically as:

- younger
- less inflamed
- less renal-disrupted
- less hepatically perturbed
- relatively platelet-preserved

The Discussion now also links this cluster to the first-encounter-only analysis, where it becomes even more frequent.

### 5. Stronger hepatic phenotype interpretation

The hepatic phenotype is now presented as:

- biologically plausible
- characterized by transaminase elevation with lower leukocytes and platelets
- potentially reflecting hepatocellular stress or mixed systemic illness
- still incompletely resolved because the panel lacks bilirubins, albumin, coagulogram, lactate, and hemodynamic variables

This improves scientific maturity while avoiding overclaiming.

### 6. Discussion improvements

The Discussion now more explicitly integrates:

- sex redistribution
- final-cohort completeness
- the meaning of the basal phenotype
- the more tentative status of the hepatic phenotype

This makes the manuscript less dependent on general clustering rhetoric and more anchored in the actual biological and data-quality results.
