# Oral Defense Narrative

## 1. How to explain the transition from the master's

The cleanest explanation is:

> My master's dissertation established the morphology intelligence layer of my research trajectory. It focused on leukocyte classification from peripheral blood smear images, but it also involved dataset construction, architecture comparison, stain-aware augmentation, external evaluation, and explicit concern with robustness. The doctorate is the natural next step: it keeps morphology as the anchor, but moves from isolated-cell recognition to case-level multimodal inference by integrating morphology with CBC parameters, analyzer flags, and structured laboratory data.

Key points to emphasize:

- this is not a thematic jump;
- the same hematology domain is preserved;
- the same morphology expertise is preserved;
- the main expansion is inferential and methodological, not opportunistic;
- the doctorate grows from the limits identified in the master's.

## 2. How to explain the scientific gap

Use a contrast-based explanation:

> The literature is already rich in image-only blood-smear classification, especially for leukocyte recognition. What remains much less developed is the integration of morphology with the other signals that actually drive hematology interpretation in practice, such as CBC parameters, analyzer flags, and structured laboratory context. So the main gap is not the lack of classifiers, but the lack of robust and interpretable case-level multimodal systems for laboratory decision support.

Then add the four-part structure if needed:

1. integration gap;
2. case-level inference gap;
3. robustness and generalization gap;
4. laboratory decision-support gap.

## 3. How to justify the multimodal approach

The best justification is practical and computational at the same time:

> In real hematology, a cell image is rarely interpreted alone. Morphology gains meaning when related to the complete blood count, analyzer alerts, and the broader laboratory context. If the goal is to build AI that is closer to real laboratory reasoning, multimodal integration is not an embellishment; it is the correct computational formulation of the problem.

Additional support points:

- it preserves the candidate's strongest prior expertise in morphology;
- it avoids remaining in a saturated image-only niche;
- it creates clearer methodological contribution for Computer Science;
- it increases translational relevance without becoming excessively clinical.

## 4. How to defend feasibility

Use a staged argument:

> The project is feasible because it is modular and builds on existing scientific assets rather than starting from zero. I already have prior work in blood-smear morphology, dataset curation, model evaluation, and robustness analysis. The doctorate is structured in incremental phases: multimodal data curation, case-level task definition, multimodal modeling, robustness analysis, and an optional constrained decision-support layer. Each phase can generate an independent publication, which reduces execution risk.

If pressed on scope:

- the core data are restricted to smear morphology, CBC, analyzer flags, and essential structured metadata;
- broader reasoning layers are optional, not mandatory;
- genomics, bone marrow, and universal platforms are explicitly outside scope.

## 5. How to explain the relevance to Computer Science

The strongest framing is:

> Although the application domain is hematology, the core research problem is computational. The thesis studies how to represent and integrate heterogeneous modalities under real-world variability, how to compare multimodal and unimodal baselines, how to evaluate calibration and uncertainty, and how to make outputs interpretable for decision support. These are Computer Science questions about data fusion, robustness, reliability, and explainable AI, investigated in a domain where they matter operationally.

Useful emphasis points:

- this is not only “AI applied to medicine”;
- it is a multimodal inference problem under domain shift;
- it has reusable methodological value beyond a single disease label;
- laboratory medicine is the translational setting, not the sole scientific contribution.

## 6. One-minute summary answer

> My doctorate proposes a natural evolution of my master's work in blood-smear morphology AI. Instead of remaining at isolated leukocyte classification, I want to develop robust and interpretable multimodal systems that integrate peripheral smear morphology with CBC parameters, analyzer flags, and structured laboratory context. The scientific gap is that this kind of case-level laboratory intelligence is still much less developed than image-only classification. The project is computationally rigorous because it centers on multimodal fusion, robustness, uncertainty, and interpretability, and it is feasible because it is modular, publication-oriented, and grounded in my previous data, methods, and laboratory experience.
