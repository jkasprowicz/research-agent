# Thesis Scope Boundaries

## Scope control principle

The thesis should be broad enough to be doctoral, but narrow enough to be executable by a full-time working researcher. The right boundary is:

**multimodal case-level hematology intelligence built around peripheral blood smear morphology plus structured laboratory data**

Everything should be judged by whether it strengthens or weakens that core.

## 1. What belongs to the core thesis

The core thesis should include:

- peripheral blood smear morphology as the anchor modality;
- continuity with leukocyte-focused morphology from the master's;
- case-level aggregation beyond isolated-cell prediction;
- integration with CBC parameters;
- integration with analyzer flags;
- selected structured laboratory metadata relevant to review or interpretation;
- multimodal fusion strategies;
- robustness, calibration, uncertainty, and interpretability;
- decision-support tasks closely tied to laboratory workflow.

Core example tasks:

- abnormal-case prioritization;
- smear-review triage;
- morphology-plus-CBC case classification;
- multimodal support for suspicious hematologic patterns;
- disagreement detection between morphology and analyzer signals.

## 2. What belongs to future expansion

These elements are valid extensions, but should not define the thesis core:

- erythrocyte morphology beyond targeted abnormalities;
- platelet morphology beyond targeted abnormalities;
- broader whole-smear abnormality inventories;
- structured expert text integration;
- retrieval-enhanced explanation layers;
- limited rule-based or knowledge-grounded reasoning;
- multicenter external validation beyond what is realistic in the available time.

These are appropriate if:

- the core multimodal thesis is already functioning;
- data quality is sufficient;
- the extension yields a publishable paper without destabilizing the main line.

## 3. What should not be attempted during the PhD

The thesis should not attempt:

- a generic LLM or agentic hematology assistant as the central identity;
- a universal laboratory AI platform;
- simultaneous integration of smear images, genomics, bone marrow, flow cytometry, and molecular diagnostics;
- a full digital hematopathology system across all specimen types;
- an excessively broad disease-agnostic diagnostic engine;
- a large prospective clinical trial unless it becomes unexpectedly feasible through institutional support;
- extensive software productization as if the PhD were a startup roadmap.

These directions are not necessarily bad. They are simply too broad, too unstable, or too disconnected from the most defensible doctoral core.

## 4. Red flags of scope explosion

If the proposal starts to include several of the items below, the scope is probably drifting:

- too many modalities added simultaneously;
- too many disease objectives in parallel;
- excessive emphasis on generic reasoning agents;
- dependence on data sources not yet secured;
- promises of real-time deployment or hospital-wide integration;
- ambitions that require a product team rather than a doctoral workflow.

## 5. Recommended scope wording for the proposal

Use wording like:

> The thesis is centered on robust and interpretable multimodal inference in peripheral blood smear hematology, integrating morphology with CBC parameters, analyzer flags, and structured laboratory context for case-level decision support. Broader reasoning or additional morphology domains are treated as conditional extensions rather than core commitments.

## 6. Practical execution boundary

To remain feasible, the thesis should aim for:

1. one well-defined multimodal dataset effort;
2. one strong multimodal modeling line;
3. one robustness/reliability line;
4. one decision-support or interpretability line.

That is enough for a strong doctorate. More than that risks fragmentation.

## Bottom line

The thesis should be:

- more than image classification;
- less than a universal hematology intelligence platform.

That middle ground is where the strongest doctorate lies.
